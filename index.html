Abstract

Gender bias in the news media has received increasing attention, and the differences introduced by gender in news coverage have been examined from a variety of perspectives. However, quotation-which is widespread in all types of news coverage-has not received enough attention. Quotes are simple, direct, and accurate reflections of the speaker's point of view, and quoted information in the news allows us to explore gender bias in the news from a different perspective.


This data story not only analyzes the preferences of different news media when quoting different gender speakers, but also analyzes the influence of different parties and nationalities on gender bias. In addition, we explored gender bias under different topics to better understand the reasons for the appearance of bias in quotations.


Do man speakers speak truly more “louder” than females? If it is true, caused by news coverage or just the gender itself? Furthermore, as time goes by, has this issue been worsened or mitigated, or just remained the same level? Let us find it out!

Track the gender bias lies in outlets, nationalities, and partisanship

In general, journalists tend to have discretion in deciding which people to bring in to provide background or analysis when doing reports, which is the most direct reason to cause the gender bias in quotations. Therefore, to do a more granular analysis of gender bias in news quotations, we chose seven of the most influential world news outlets to track their gender preferences and evolving trends in quotes.


In addition, we analyzed, in the same way, whether the nationality and party affiliation of the speaker introduces a gender bias in the citation. In other words, whether being in a different country or party affects the probability of women speaking out.

Analyze the gender bias with different topics

Obviously, the words of different genders may have different exposure under different news topics, so analyzing the gender ratio of quoted speakers under different topics helps us to conduct a more fine-grained analysis. We manually selected 9 common topics in the news and defined keywords for each of these 9 topics in order to extract references to the corresponding topics from the data. In addition, we analyzed the sentiment of the sentences to study the difference in sentiment between men and women on specific topics.

Detecting the linguistic differences between men and women in quotation

To better understand the differences in word usage between men and women in the news, we randomly selected 1 million citations for each gender sequentially from 2015 to 2020, thus constituting a balanced training data of 12 million citations. After that, we use the bag-of-words model as well as TF-IDF to convert the quotation into vectors and select the top 2,000 high-frequency words as features to predict the speaker's gender. The prediction model chosen is a logistic regression model.
