Exploring explicit and implicit gender bias

from news quotations

“Gender bias does worldwide damage. It’s a cause of low productivity on farms. It’s a source of poverty and disease. It’s at the core of social customs that keep women down.”



--Melinda Gates
The Moment of Lift: How Empowering Women Changes the World

What can we do from quotations?

Gender bias in the news media has received increasing attention, and the differences introduced by gender in news coverage have been examined from a variety of perspectives. However, quotation–which is widespread in all types of news coverage–has not received enough attention. Quotes are simple, direct, and accurate reflections of the speaker's point of view, and quoted information in the news allows us to explore gender bias in the news from a fresh angle. Thus, we then introduce a new set of questions: Do male speakers speak truly more “louder” than females? If it is true, is this caused by news coverage or just the gender itself? Furthermore, as time goes by, has this issue been worsened or mitigated, or just remained at the same level?

As a result, our data story not only analyzes the preferences of various news media when quoting speakers from different genders, but also the influence of numerous parties and nationalities on gender bias. In addition, we explore gender bias under self-defined topics to better understand the reasons behind the appearance of bias in quotations.

Gender bias in quotations: men’s voice is indeed louder than women(three times larger!)

We display three types of gender percentages from January 1, 2015, to April 30, 2020, considering a relatively small number of sexual minorities, we grouped them to “others”. We decide to narrow the scope of this experiment by assessing only binary gender representations. (Occurrence means the total number of quotations that appear in the media. Speaker is the number of different speakers in the dataset. Quotation represents the number of different quotations.) At the first glance, you can easily observe that over 80% of speakers quoted by outlets are men, which is consistent with other related work on gender bias. [1], [2] [3]. You might be misled to believe that there are many more males than females on earth, however, it is totally wrong for the whole people in the world or even just for citizens in the USA(for both the gender ratio is about 1:1). Interestingly, the percentage of women speakers is about 5% higher than the other two types of aggregations, which means that though women accounted for nearly a quarter of the whole speakers, they were still less quoted by news outlets. The under-representation of women in society in general and in news articles reflect that we are far from gender equality.

To be more concrete, we display Annual Top 10 quoted speakers from 2015 to 2019. As we can see(without any surprise), men have dominated the quotation in news: Men accounted for the majority of quotes in the top 10.

According to the Interparliamentary Union, 77 per cent of the world’s parliamentarians are male, and only two out of 193 parliaments (in Rwanda and Bolivia) comprise at least 50 per cent of women.

The majority of this dataset(i.e. QuoteBank) is from news media, where politics is always the hot spot. However, women are not in a dominant position in politics generally. Thus, it is reasonable to observe that most speakers in the Top 10 are males, and the amount of quotations from females is far below those of males.

What if we control possible causation, is the bias still existing? Let’s say, Hillary Clinton and Donald Trump. From the dataset, the amount of the quotation by Donald Trump is about three times larger than that by Hillary Clinton. However, they were both the competitors in President Election 2016, the difference of their exposure on social media and their influence(even the final vote results) cannot make such a big impact on quotation amount. The only possible factor leading to such a bias might be from news outlets and even society.

Insights on gender bias in outlets, nationalities, and partisanship

There needs to be a fundamental shift in the way societies view women in government, one that does not see them as mere seat-fillers or stats on a chart, they must be viewed as a vital contributing factor to the betterment of the world.

Aysha Taryam

In general, journalists have discretion over choosing whom to provide background or analysis when doing reports, and those people remain overwhelmingly male. This could be the most direct reason to cause gender bias in quotations. Therefore, to do a more granular analysis of gender bias in news quotations, we select seven of the most influential world news outlets and tracked their gender preferences and evolving trends in quotes.

In addition, we investigate, in the same way, whether the nationality and party affiliation of the speaker introduces a gender bias in the citation. In other words, whether being in a different country or party affects the chance of women speaking out.

Females are in the disadvantaged situation generally in news outlets, especially terrible in CNBC

First, for news outlets over these 5 years, it is easy to see that in Fox News the number of male speakers exceeds that of other media by a large amount, and it also achieves a staggering amount–over 14 million! If we focus on the male-female ratio, CNBC performs the “best score”–85.72% for men(but it is not a good thing). The bias could be much worse in 2020 when males take up 89.6%.


At a macro level, female speakers are always in the disadvantaged position(whatever the news outlet is) so that the percentage variances among news outlets are not significantly different(highly consistent trends). This phenomenon inspires us that media preferences themselves may have little impact on the production of gender bias. In other words, they are simply a truthful reflection of the neglect of women that lurks in society.


Although women do not appear in the news as often as men, the percentage of women did increase from 2015 to 2020, we made some progress.

The bias is also obvious(and terrible) among nationalities

How about the cases of nationalities? It is such a pity to say that in all countries, the above phenomenon is the same. Though more females are quoted in the USA(compared to other countries), it doesn’t mean that there is less bias in America, because almost all the news outlets are from America. In terms of period, the percentage of female speakers is generally lower in France than in other countries.


What’s the influence that the female leaders have on women’s voices? Will it be “louder”? Let’s take a detailed look at the period from mid-2016 to early 2017 and Britain could be suitable evidence. When Theresa May became the Prime Minister of the United Kingdom, the percentage of female quotations in England had risen to 23.76%. As a result, female leaders can have a greater impact on mitigating the existing gender bias compared to males.


According to the Human Development Index(HDI) assessment published by the United Nations Development Programme, India's development index (0.645) is much lower than other developed countries in the data (e.g. 0.926 in the United States and 0.901 in France), yet there has been no significant progress or improvement in the percentage of women's voices. From this, we can infer that the level of development of countries doesn’t have a noticeable impact on reducing gender bias.

Partisanship: Something different?

The case is kind of different compared to news outlets and nationalities. There is also an obvious bias in the Republican Party(88.35% is male) but in the Democratic Party, female speakers occupy 34.03%. This observation might be explained by the aims of the Democratic Party.

“We are committed to ensuring full equality for women. Democrats will fight to end gender discrimination in the areas of education, employment, health care, or any other sphere. We will combat biases across economic, political, and social life that hold women back and limit their opportunities.”

We know that Democratic Party tends to support female candidates and defend the legitimate interests of minorities(e.g. female, racial minorities…), which implies that it encourages females to speak out. It also reflects the potential difference between the Democratic and Republican Parties regarding feminism issues. Therefore, this truth is consistent with our statistical finding: there are several most-quoted female speakers in the Democratic Party including Nancy Pelosi, Elizabeth Warren, Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez and Kamala Harris.

Do topics reveal potential gender bias? How?

A gender-equal society would be one where the word ‘gender’ does not exist: where everyone can be themselves.

Gloria Steinem

Obviously, the words used by males or females may have different exposure under various news topics, so exploring the gender ratio of quoted speakers under different topics can help us conduct a more fine-grained analysis. We manually select 9 common topics in the news and define keywords for each of these topics to extract related quotations from the data. In addition, we examine three sentiments(Positive, Negative and Neutral) of the sentences to study the distributions in sentiments between men and women on specific topics.

Women seem to be sensual and benevolent, men seem to be aspiring and sports-focused

We first draw the evolution of women speakers’ percentage per month for each topic, and these topics cover a wide range of areas commonly seen in the news, as shown in the vertical axis of the figure. As we can see, news organizations cover male or female voices differently, depending on the topics. For females, quotations related to education, health, lifestyle, and entertainment are more frequent than the average level. Whereas in politics, people, business and sports, women percentages are below the global average as shown in the first graph above. Looking at the topics with a predominantly male quotation count, it is easy to see that these careers tend to be dominated by men. Take sports for example, according to the Forbes 2021 ranking of the top 50 highest-paid athletes, the highest-ranked woman: Naomi Osaka, is only 12th. And her annual income of $60M is only 1/3 of the first place sports star Conor McGregor.

To our surprise, under the topic of gender, the percentage of women speaking out is at its highest level, but their voices are just almost equal to men's(still less).

To summarize briefly, female quotation sources are more often in caregiving roles and male quotations are more in sporting and business roles, reflecting the position of women as caregivers but men as leaders and breadwinners.

Sentiment Analysis for specific Topics

Text description

Let’s first look at gender. You can notice that for some sorrowful topics, such as “abortion” and “sexual harassment”, both men and women tend to express a negative sentiment, which may imply that these social problems have not been improved or solved significantly in society.

How about entertainment? It is interesting to find that females tend to be positive about “art”, while men are not(40.53% are negative). But in terms of “music”, only 9.46% of men have a negative attitude. It looks that males prefer “music” more than “art”(Perhaps art is boring?).

The most popular keyword in sports for males is “football”, extremely larger than others, especially for “swimming”, which is consistent with our normal perceptions. When people talk about football games, the first image must be male athletes running on the field. For women, they prefer “tennis” more(even greater than such a common and popular topic, “football”). In other words, football is more “male-like” than tennis.

In our conclusion, for some specific topics, such as ‘art’ and ‘tennis’, we can indeed observe a visible difference. However, not what one would expect, for most common topics men and women show a similar attitude distribution.

Detecting the linguistic differences between men and women in quotation

We have already known that males and females tend to show different preferences towards various topics(from heatmap and sentiment analysis). Now, we wonder: can we predict the gender(i.e. male or female) when we know the quotations?

If we cannot infer it just from what they say(worse than random prediction), it means that the quotation style discrepancy between males and females is not obvious as we expected.

How do we design such a machine learning model?

The prediction model chosen is a logistic regression model, for it is actually a binary-classification task. We randomly select 1 million citations for each gender sequentially from 2015 to 2020, constituting a balanced training data of 12 million citations. After that, we use the bag-of-words model as well as TF-IDF to convert the quotation into vectors and select the top 2,000 most frequent words as features to predict the speaker's gender.

If there were no significant differences in language between men and women, then the model would perform as equivalent to a random guess, i.e., 50%. However, if the model performs better than 50%(in our task R-Square is 59.65%), it indicates that there is indeed some bias in quotations spoken by different genders, which is thus identified by the model as a discriminative feature.

How can we infer the gender bias from the weights in the model?

In this binary-classification task, we label females as 0, males as 1. Therefore, the larger the parameters are, the more male-oriented the corresponding word is. Conversely, the smaller the parameters are, the corresponding words are more “female”. As a result, this logistic regression model provides us with a way to probe those predictive words.

The figure below shows the top 50 most predictive words for males and females, where we can see that the representative words for male quotes are dominated by sports and business-related words, which is in line with our previous finds in Heatmap. Therefore, this model verifies our observation: men are indeed more “ambitious” and “active”. In fact, in a long time period, these areas(e.g. business, sports, politics) tend to be dominated by males, who are already more in numbers in society, not to mention the quotations. For women, it is more about their own characteristics and some life-like words, which is also consistent with our previous analysis. This reflects the reality of women in the social division of labour: being a good wife, a good mother, and focusing on life rather than dominating a particular industry.

Our finding: Gender bias indeed exists! There is a long way to go towards gender equality!
